Q1 – Matrix Multiplication

You're given two grids of numbers and asked to "multiply" them. It looks scary the first time, but it's really just one tiny operation — the dot product — repeated a few times. Let's build it up slowly.

What even is a matrix?

A matrix is just a rectangular grid of numbers arranged in rows (going across →) and columns (going down ↓). That's it. No magic.

We describe its size as (rows × columns). So a grid with 3 rows and 1 column is a (3×1); one row and three columns is a (1×3); the square one with 9 numbers is a (3×3).

1Naming a position inside the grid

We write $M_{ij}$ to mean "the number in row $i$, column $j$". Row number first, column number second — always.

$$M=\begin{pmatrix} M_{11} & M_{12} & M_{13} \\ M_{21} & M_{22} & M_{23} \\ M_{31} & M_{32} & M_{33}\end{pmatrix}$$
💡
Think of it like a spreadsheet$M_{23}$ is "row 2, column 3" — exactly how you'd find a cell in Excel. Row, then column.
2The ONE operation behind everything: the dot product

Every matrix multiplication is built from a single move called the dot product. Here's the whole idea:

In plain wordsTake two lists of numbers of the same length. Multiply them position-by-position (1st×1st, 2nd×2nd, …), then add all those products into one single number.
$$[a_1,a_2,a_3]\cdot[b_1,b_2,b_3]=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3$$
Worked example — Jan 2026 Q1

$\left[-\tfrac{5}{9},\ \tfrac{1}{3},\ \tfrac{5}{9}\right]\cdot[0,\,-2,\,3]$

Multiply matching positions: $\left(-\tfrac{5}{9}\right)(0)$, then $\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)(-2)$, then $\left(\tfrac{5}{9}\right)(3)$
$=0\ +\ \left(-\tfrac{2}{3}\right)\ +\ \tfrac{15}{9}$
$\tfrac{15}{9}=\tfrac{5}{3}$, so $0-\tfrac{2}{3}+\tfrac{5}{3}=\tfrac{3}{3}=$ 1
⚠️
Fraction trap$\left(-\tfrac{5}{9}\right)\times 3=-\tfrac{15}{9}=-\tfrac{5}{3}$, NOT $-\tfrac{5}{3}$ by "cancelling the 3 with the 9 wrongly". Work one step at a time and keep the denominator.
3The size rule — can these two even be multiplied?

Before doing anything, write the two sizes side by side. Matrix multiplication only works when the inner numbers match, and the result takes the outer numbers:

$$(m\times \underbrace{n)\cdot(n}_{\text{must match}}\times p)=(m\times p)$$
Why the inner numbers must matchMultiplying means dot-producting a row of the left with a column of the right. A row of the left has $n$ entries; a column of the right has $n$ entries. To pair them up position-by-position, both must have the same length $n$. If they don't match, the multiplication is simply undefined.
🧩
Think of it like puzzle piecesThe two inner edges have to be the same shape to click together. The leftover outer edges become the shape of your answer.

The 3 Cases You Will Actually See

Across every past paper, Q1 is always one of these three. Once you spot which case it is, you know exactly what to do.

Case 1 — Column × Row $=$ a full matrix   $(3\times1)(1\times3)\to(3\times3)$

Each number in the column "scales" the entire row. Result entry $(i,j)=\text{column}_i\times\text{row}_j$.

$$\begin{pmatrix}a\\b\\c\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}d&e&f\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}ad&ae&af\\bd&be&bf\\cd&ce&cf\end{pmatrix}$$
Worked — Jan 2025

$M=\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\\1\end{pmatrix}$,   $N=\begin{pmatrix}1&c&1\end{pmatrix}$. Multiply each row-number of $M$ into $N$:

Row 1 (from $1$): $1\cdot[1,c,1]=[1,c,1]$  •  Row 2 (from $3$): $[3,3c,3]$  •  Row 3 (from $1$): $[1,c,1]$

$$M\cdot N=\begin{pmatrix}1&c&1\\3&3c&3\\1&c&1\end{pmatrix}$$

Case 2 — Row × Column $=$ a single number   $(1\times3)(3\times1)\to$ scalar

This is literally just one dot product. Multiply position-by-position, add them up, done.

$$[a,b,c]\begin{pmatrix}d\\e\\f\end{pmatrix}=ad+be+cf$$
How to spot itA flat row on the left, a tall column on the right → your answer is one number, not a grid.

Case 3 — full $(3\times3)(3\times2)\to(3\times2)$

Same idea, just more of it. Each answer entry $(i,j)$ is "row $i$ of the left, dotted with column $j$ of the right". A $3\times2$ answer has 6 entries, so you do 6 dot products.

Q2 – Logarithms & Exponentials

You're given a function and a point, and asked for the value there. The whole trick is recognising that logs and exponentials are opposite operations that cancel each other — like adding then subtracting the same number.

What is a logarithm, really?

A logarithm answers one question: "what power do I need?"

$\log_b(x)$ asks: "What power must I raise $b$ to, in order to get $x$?"

For example $\log_2(8)=3$, because $2^3=8$. And $\log_{10}(1000)=3$, because $10^3=1000$. The log just hands you back the exponent.

1Two flavours: log and ln
  • $\log$ (no base written) usually means base $10$ in economics.
  • $\ln$ means base $e$, where $e\approx 2.718\ldots$ — a special constant like $\pi$.

Good news: they obey exactly the same rules. Anything true for $\ln$ is true for $\log$.

2Why $e$ and $\ln$ are best friends

$e^x$ and $\ln x$ are inverse functions — each one undoes the other, like a lock and its key.

$$e^{\ln x}=x \qquad\text{and}\qquad \ln(e^x)=x$$
In plain wordsIf you take the log of something and then exponentiate it (or vice-versa), you get back exactly what you started with. Nothing left to calculate.
🔑
This is the most useful trick in the whole courseThe moment you see $e^{\ln(\ldots)}$ or $\ln(e^{\ldots})$, cross them out and keep what's inside. Do this before any other step.
$y=\ln x$ (blue) and $y=e^{x}$ (green) are mirror images across the line $y=x$ — that's what "inverse" looks like

Every Identity — With the Reason

Don't memorise blindly. Each rule has a one-line reason; once you see it, you won't forget it.

$\ln(1)=0$ and $\log(1)=0$

Why$\log_b(1)$ asks "what power gives $1$?" — and any base to the power $0$ is $1$ ($b^0=1$). So the answer is $0$.

Exam use: the instant you see $\log(1)$ or $\ln(1)$ anywhere, replace it with $0$.

$\ln(ab)=\ln a+\ln b$   (product becomes sum)

WhyBecause $e^A\cdot e^B=e^{A+B}$ — multiplying powers adds the exponents. Logs read off exponents, so a product turns into a sum.

$\ln\!\left(\tfrac{a}{b}\right)=\ln a-\ln b$   (division becomes subtraction)

Special case worth knowing: $\ln\!\left(\tfrac{1}{x}\right)=\ln 1-\ln x=-\ln x$.

$\ln(a^n)=n\,\ln a$   (powers come down to the front)

Example: $\ln(x^2)=2\ln x$. And combined with cancellation: $\ln(e^{3x})=3x$.

❌
The rule that does NOT exist$\log(a+b)\neq\log a+\log b$. Logs only break apart multiplication, never addition. There is simply no rule for $\log$ of a sum.

Q3 – Domain of a Function

The domain is just the list of inputs a function is allowed to take. Some inputs break the maths — the domain is everything except those broken values. Let's see exactly what "breaks" a function.

Why does a domain even exist?

A function is a machine: you feed in a number, it gives one back. But some numbers jam the machine and produce nonsense. There are exactly three things that jam it:

  • Square root of a negative — $\sqrt{-4}$ isn't a real number.
  • Log of zero or a negative — $\log(0)$ and $\log(-1)$ aren't defined.
  • Dividing by zero — $\tfrac{1}{0}$ is undefined.

So the domain = "all real numbers, minus the inputs that trigger one of those three problems." Finding the domain means finding and excluding the troublemakers.

The 4 Rules (each with the reason)

Rule 1 — $\sqrt{f(x)}$ needs $f(x)\geq 0$

You can square-root zero or a positive number, but not a negative one.

⚠
The exceptionIf the root sits in a denominator, like $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{f(x)}}$, you need $f(x)>0$ strictly — because $\sqrt{0}=0$ would mean dividing by zero.

Rule 2 — $\ln f(x)$ or $\log f(x)$ needs $f(x)>0$

Why$\log$ asks "what power gives this value?" There's no power of $10$ that produces $0$ or a negative number, so the log of those simply doesn't exist. The inside must be strictly positive.

Rule 3 — $\tfrac{1}{f(x)}$ needs $f(x)\neq 0$

Dividing by zero is undefined, so throw out every $x$ that makes the denominator zero.

Rule 4 — $\tfrac{1}{\ln f(x)}$ needs TWO things at once

This one stacks two rules:

  • The $\ln$ must exist: $f(x)>0$.
  • The denominator can't be zero: $\ln f(x)\neq 0$, which means $f(x)\neq 1$ (since $\ln 1=0$).

Combined: $f(x)>0$ and $f(x)\neq 1$.

This was Jan 2025 Q3

$f(x)=\dfrac{1}{\ln(x^2+1)}$. Here $x^2+1>0$ for every $x$ (automatically fine). But $\ln(x^2+1)=0$ when $x^2+1=1$, i.e. $x=0$ — exclude it.

$$D_f=\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$$

The Sign-Chart Method (for "polynomial $>0$")

When a rule gives you something like "$x^3-x>0$", you need to know where that polynomial is positive. The sign chart is a clean, foolproof way to find out.

🚦

Factor the polynomial completely.

Test one point inside each interval — is the result $+$ or $-$?
Read off the intervals matching your condition.

Q4 – Derivatives

A derivative measures how fast something is changing. Geometrically it's the slope of the curve at a point. You'll learn four rules — power, chain, product, quotient — and one habit (simplify first) that saves you minutes.

What is a derivative? (the picture)

$f'(x)$ is the slope of the curve at the point $x$ — how steep it is and which way it tilts.

  • $f'(x)>0$ → curve going up (increasing).
  • $f'(x)<0$ → curve going down (decreasing).
  • $f'(x)=0$ → flat tangent — a peak, a valley, or a pause (a critical point).
1Power rule — the workhorse
$$\frac{d}{dx}\big[x^n\big]=n\,x^{n-1}$$
In plain wordsBring the exponent down to the front, then knock the exponent down by one.

Examples: $\tfrac{d}{dx}[x^3]=3x^2$,   $\tfrac{d}{dx}[x]=1$,   $\tfrac{d}{dx}[\text{constant}]=0$.

2Chain rule — for a function inside a function
$$\frac{d}{dx}\big[f(g(x))\big]=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$
🪆
Think of it like Russian dollsDifferentiate the outer shell (leaving the inside untouched), then multiply by the derivative of what's inside. "Derivative of the outside times derivative of the inside."

Here it is at three difficulty levels so you can see the pattern grow:

LevelFunctionOuter · Inner′Result

3Product rule — for two things multiplied
$$(uv)'=u'v+uv'$$
In plain words"Derivative of the first times the second, PLUS the first times derivative of the second." You need both terms.
⚠️
Don't just differentiate each piece$\tfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 e^x]\neq 2x\,e^x$. That's missing a term. Correct: $2x\,e^x+x^2 e^x=e^x(x^2+2x)$.
4Quotient rule — for one thing divided by another
$$\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)'=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$$
🎵

⚠️

Worked — Jan 2025 Q4

$f(x)=\dfrac{2x^2}{x^2+2}$.   $u=2x^2\Rightarrow u'=4x$;   $v=x^2+2\Rightarrow v'=2x$.

$f'=\dfrac{4x(x^2+2)-2x^2(2x)}{(x^2+2)^2}=\dfrac{4x^3+8x-4x^3}{(x^2+2)^2}$

The $4x^3$ terms cancel — always expand and check!

$$f'(x)=\dfrac{8x}{(x^2+2)^2}$$
💡

Q5 – The Second Derivative

The second derivative $f''$ is simply "the derivative of the derivative". If $f'$ tells you the slope, $f''$ tells you how the slope is changing — i.e. how the curve bends.

What does $f''$ tell you?

  • $f''(x)>0$ → concave up (curves like a bowl 🥣).
  • $f''(x)<0$ → concave down (curves like a hill ⛰️).
  • $f''(x)=0$ and the sign flips → an inflection point (the bend switches direction).

The min/max test (you'll use this in Q7 and Exercise A too)

At a flat point where $f'(x^*)=0$:

  • $f''(x^*)>0$ → it's a local minimum (bowl holds water).
  • $f''(x^*)<0$ → it's a local maximum (hilltop).
  • $f''(x^*)=0$ → inconclusive, look closer.
⚠️
The #1 Q5 mistakeAfter you compute $f'$, it is almost always a product of two things. To get $f''$ you must use the product rule — forgetting this drops a whole term.
Worked in full — $f(x)=e^{-4x^2}$ (Mar/Dec 2025)
First derivative (chain rule). Outer $e^u$, inner $u=-4x^2$ with $u'=-8x$:
$$f'(x)=-8x\,e^{-4x^2}$$
Notice $f'$ is a product: $(-8x)\times e^{-4x^2}$. So use the product rule with $u=-8x\ (u'=-8)$ and $v=e^{-4x^2}\ (v'=-8x\,e^{-4x^2})$.
Apply $u'v+uv'$: $$f''=(-8)e^{-4x^2}+(-8x)(-8x\,e^{-4x^2})=-8e^{-4x^2}+64x^2e^{-4x^2}$$
Factor out $e^{-4x^2}$:
$$f''(x)=e^{-4x^2}\,(64x^2-8)$$
Why factor out $e^{-4x^2}$?Because $e^{\text{anything}}>0$ always, it never changes the sign. So the sign of $f''$ is entirely decided by $(64x^2-8)$ — handy if you later need where the curve changes concavity.

The reusable pattern: $f(x)=e^{ax^2}$

$$f'(x)=2ax\,e^{ax^2}\qquad f''(x)=e^{ax^2}\,(4a^2x^2+2a)$$

Memorise this shape and most Q5s become a 20-second fill-in-the-constant exercise.

Q6 – Determinant & Trace

From a square matrix you can pull out two single numbers: the trace (super easy) and the determinant (a bit more work, but loaded with shortcuts). This question is pure free marks once you know the tricks.

1Trace — just add the diagonal

The diagonal runs top-left to bottom-right. The trace is the sum of those entries. That's the whole thing.

$$\text{tr}(C)=C_{11}+C_{22}+C_{33}$$
Bonus fact you'll use in Q8The trace also equals the sum of the eigenvalues: $\text{tr}(H)=\lambda_1+\lambda_2$.
2Determinant — what it means

The determinant is one number that "summarises" a matrix.

  • $\det\neq0$ → the matrix is well-behaved (invertible).
  • $\det=0$ → the matrix is singular — its rows are secretly dependent (one is a combo of the others).

For a $2\times2$ it's just one cross-multiplication:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}=ad-bc$$

Why it matters here: in Exercise B, the determinant of the Hessian tells you whether a critical point is a min, max, or saddle.

The 5 shortcuts — CHECK THESE FIRST

Before grinding through a full expansion, glance for one of these. Often you can write the answer in five seconds:

A row (or column) of all zeros → $\det=0$. Done.
Two identical rows → $\det=0$. Done.
Two proportional rows (one is $k\times$ another) → $\det=0$.
Triangular matrix (zeros all above or all below the diagonal) → $\det=$ product of the diagonal.
Otherwise, expand along the row/column with the most zeros to minimise work.
Shortcuts in action

Jan 2025: $\begin{pmatrix}3&2&3\\0&0&0\\0&3&0\end{pmatrix}$ — row 2 is all zeros → $\det=\boxed0$ instantly. Trace $=3+0+0=\boxed3$.

Jan 2026: $\begin{pmatrix}1&\pi&1\\1&-2&1\\1&\pi&1\end{pmatrix}$ — row 1 $=$ row 3 → $\det=\boxed0$ instantly. Trace $=1-2+1=\boxed0$.

3Cofactor expansion — the full method (when no shortcut applies)

Expand along row 1 (or any row/column). Each entry multiplies the little $2\times2$ determinant left after you delete its row and column — with alternating signs:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{pmatrix}=a(ei-fh)-b(di-fg)+c(dh-eg)$$
➕➖
The sign patternRow 1 signs go $+\ -\ +$. (The full checkerboard is $\begin{smallmatrix}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+\end{smallmatrix}$.) Pick the row/column with the most zeros so the $-$ and $+$ terms with a zero just vanish.

Q7 – Local Minimum / Maximum

A peak or a valley on a curve always has one thing in common: the curve is momentarily flat there. So the recipe is "find where the slope is zero, then check whether it's a valley or a peak."

The idea in one picture

At the very bottom of a valley (a minimum) or the very top of a hill (a maximum), the tangent line is horizontal — the slope is $0$. So we hunt for points where $f'(x)=0$, then decide which kind they are.

1The general 3-step method
Differentiate: find $f'(x)$.

Classify with $f''$: $f''>0$ → minimum (bowl), $f''<0$ → maximum (hill).

Q8 – Eigenvalues of the Hessian

Don't be scared by the word "eigenvalue". For this exam it means a couple of special numbers attached to a $2\times2$ matrix called the Hessian — and there's a shortcut that makes most of them instant.

What is the Hessian?

For a function $f(x,y)$, the Hessian is the $2\times2$ grid of its second partial derivatives. It captures how the surface curves in each direction.

$$H=\begin{pmatrix}f_{xx}&f_{xy}\\f_{xy}&f_{yy}\end{pmatrix}$$

(It's symmetric: the top-right and bottom-left are both $f_{xy}$.)

1The general formula

The two eigenvalues come from this formula (it uses the trace and determinant):

$$\lambda_{1,2}=\frac{\text{tr}(H)\pm\sqrt{\text{tr}(H)^2-4\det(H)}}{2}$$
2The shortcut that saves you every time
If $f_{xy}=0$ (a diagonal Hessian), the eigenvalues ARE just the diagonal entries. No formula needed — read them straight off as $f_{xx}$ and $f_{yy}$.
When is $f_{xy}=0$?Whenever the function has no mixed $xy$ term — for example $f=x^2-y^2-2x-2y$. Differentiating $f_x$ with respect to $y$ gives $0$. These "separable" functions show up constantly.
Worked — Jan 2025 Q8

$f(x,y)=x^2-y^2-2x-2y$. Then $f_{xx}=2$, $f_{yy}=-2$, $f_{xy}=0$.

Diagonal Hessian → read off the eigenvalues directly. (The point they ask about is irrelevant here because $H$ is constant.)

$$\lambda_1=2,\qquad \lambda_2=-2$$

Exercise A – Analysing a Function

You're handed one function and asked to understand it completely — where it lives, how it behaves at the edges, where it rises and falls, how it bends, and finally to sketch it. It's the same 5-step recipe every single time. Master the recipe and this exercise is yours.

The 5-step recipe (memorise this order)

Domain — where is the function allowed to exist? (the 4 rules from Q3)
Limits & asymptotes — what happens at the edges of the domain and at $\pm\infty$?
First derivative $f'$ — sign chart → where it rises/falls → maxima & minima.
Second derivative $f''$ — sign chart → concavity & inflection points.
Sketch — combine everything into one clean picture.

Step 2 in depth: Limits & Asymptotes

A limit asks: "as $x$ creeps toward some value, what number does $f(x)$ head for?" It doesn't matter whether $f$ is actually defined there — we only care about the trend.

The four limits you'll take

  • $x\to+\infty$ and $x\to-\infty$ — behaviour far out left and right.
  • $x\to a^{+}$ (from the right) and $x\to a^{-}$ (from the left) — behaviour near a "forbidden" point $a$.

What the answer tells you

  • Limit $=$ a finite number $L$ → horizontal asymptote $y=L$ (the curve flattens toward that height).
  • Limit $=\pm\infty$ at a point $x=a$ → vertical asymptote $x=a$ (the curve shoots up/down a wall there).
🏃 The growth-rate hierarchy (memorise!)

When $x$ gets huge, some functions grow much faster than others. From slowest to fastest:

$$\log x \;\ll\; x^n \;\ll\; e^x$$
What this buys you$\dfrac{x^n}{e^x}\to 0$ (exponential crushes any polynomial) and $\dfrac{\log x}{x^n}\to 0$ (polynomial beats logarithm). So in a race to infinity, whoever's further right wins.

Rational functions (poly ÷ poly) as $x\to\pm\infty$

  • Same top & bottom degree → limit $=$ ratio of leading coefficients.
  • Bigger degree on top → limit $=\pm\infty$.
  • Bigger degree on bottom → limit $=0$.
🧮 L'Hôpital's Rule — for "stuck" limits

Sometimes plugging in gives a nonsense form like $\tfrac{0}{0}$ or $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ — called indeterminate. The rule: differentiate the top and the bottom separately, then try the limit again. Repeat if still stuck.

$$\lim\frac{f}{g}=\lim\frac{f'}{g'}\quad(\text{only for }\tfrac00\text{ or }\tfrac{\infty}{\infty})$$
⚠
Only when indeterminateIf the limit is already a clear number (like $\tfrac53$) or clearly infinite, do NOT use L'Hôpital — it'll give the wrong answer.
Worked — Jan 2025 Exercise A

$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to+\infty}\frac{5x^2-2x}{e^x}$. Plugging in gives $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ — indeterminate.

Round 1: $\dfrac{10x-2}{e^x}$ — still $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$.
Round 2: $\dfrac{10}{e^x}\to\dfrac{10}{\infty}=0$.
Result: limit $=0$ → horizontal asymptote $y=0$ on the right. (Or just quote the hierarchy: $e^x$ beats $x^2$.)

Steps 3 & 4 in depth: Sign Charts

A sign chart is a tidy table showing where $f'$ (or $f''$) is positive or negative. It converts "solve a derivative" into "read the shape of the curve".

Reading $f'$ (slope)

  • $+$ region → $f$ increasing ↗️
  • $-$ region → $f$ decreasing ↘️
  • $+$ to $-$ → local maximum
  • $-$ to $+$ → local minimum
  • no sign change → inflection (not an extremum)

Reading $f''$ (bend)

  • $+$ region → concave up (bowl)
  • $-$ region → concave down (arch)
  • sign change → inflection point
Worked — a sneaky case (Jul 2025: $f=\tfrac{x^3}{x^3+1}$)

$f'(x)=\dfrac{3x^2}{(x^3+1)^2}$. The numerator $3x^2\geq0$ and the denominator is squared $\geq0$, so $f'\geq0$ everywhere in the domain.

Conclusion: $f$ is non-decreasing throughout — no max or min. At $x=0$, $f'=0$ but the sign doesn't flip, so it's an inflection point, not an extremum.

Exercise B – 2D Optimisation

Now the function has two inputs, $g(x,y)$ — picture a landscape of hills and valleys. Your job: find every flat spot (critical point) and decide whether each is a valley bottom (min), a hilltop (max), or a mountain pass (saddle).

The 6-step recipe

Domain — usually all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, unless there's a log or square root.
Gradient — compute the partial derivatives $g_x$ and $g_y$.
Critical points — solve $g_x=0$ AND $g_y=0$ together. Find them ALL.
Hessian — the matrix of second partials $g_{xx},g_{yy},g_{xy}$.
Classify each point using $\det H$ and the sign of $g_{xx}$.
Taylor — write the 2nd-order approximation at each critical point.

Step 2 in depth: Partial Derivatives

A partial derivative asks how $g$ changes when you wiggle one variable and hold the other perfectly still.

The whole technique in one lineTo get $g_x$: treat $y$ as a frozen constant and differentiate in $x$ as normal. To get $g_y$: freeze $x$ and differentiate in $y$.
Worked — separable, Jan 2025

$g=x^3-3x+y^3-3y$. Freezing $y$, the whole $y^3-3y$ is a constant (derivative $0$):

$g_x=3x^2-3$  •  $g_y=3y^2-3$.

Worked — with chain rule, Mar 2026

$g=\log(1-x^2-y^2)$. Using $\tfrac{d}{dx}\log u=\tfrac{u'}{u\ln10}$:

$g_x=\dfrac{-2x}{(1-x^2-y^2)\ln10}$  •  $g_y=\dfrac{-2y}{(1-x^2-y^2)\ln10}$.

Step 3 in depth: Finding ALL Critical Points

Set both partials to zero and solve the system. There are three reliable strategies:

Strategy 1 — Separable

If $g=f(x)+h(y)$ (no $xy$ term), the two equations are independent. Solve $g_x=0$ for $x$ alone, $g_y=0$ for $y$ alone, then pair up every combination.

⚡
If $g_x=0$ has 3 solutions and $g_y=0$ has 3, you get $3\times3=\textbf{9}$ critical points. Don't miss any!

Strategy 2 — Symmetry

If swapping $x$ and $y$ leaves the system unchanged, try $x=y$ first — it usually cracks it open.

Strategy 3 — Substitution

Solve one equation for one variable, plug into the other.

Worked (symmetry) — May 2025

$g=\tfrac{x^3}{3}+\tfrac{y^3}{3}-x^2-y^2-3xy+2$.   $g_x=x^2-2x-3y=0$,   $g_y=y^2-2y-3x=0$.

The system is symmetric, so try $x=y$: $x^2-2x-3x=0\Rightarrow x^2-5x=0\Rightarrow x(x-5)=0$. So $x=0$ or $x=5$.

Critical points: $(0,0)$ and $(5,5)$ (both verified in the second equation).

Step 4 in depth: The Hessian

The Hessian collects all four second partials:

$$H=\begin{pmatrix}g_{xx}&g_{xy}\\g_{xy}&g_{yy}\end{pmatrix}$$
  • $g_{xx}$: differentiate $g_x$ again in $x$.
  • $g_{yy}$: differentiate $g_y$ again in $y$.
  • $g_{xy}$: differentiate $g_x$ in $y$ (you get the same answer differentiating $g_y$ in $x$).
Shortcut$g_{xy}=0$ exactly when there's no $xy$ cross-term. Separable functions always give a diagonal Hessian — which makes the next step trivial.

Step 5 in depth: Classifying (the decision tree)

Compute $\det H=g_{xx}g_{yy}-g_{xy}^2$, then follow the branches:

Compute $\det H=g_{xx}g_{yy}-g_{xy}^{\,2}$
$\det H<0$ → SADDLE POINT (mountain pass)
$\det H>0$ and $g_{xx}>0$ → LOCAL MIN (bowl up)
$\det H>0$ and $g_{xx}<0$ → LOCAL MAX (bowl down)
$\det H=0$ → INCONCLUSIVE (need more work)
Why the determinant decides thisFor a $2\times2$ matrix, $\det=$ product of the two eigenvalues, and eigenvalues are the curvatures along the principal directions. $\det<0$ means the curvatures have opposite signs — up one way, down the other — that's a saddle, always. $\det>0$ means same sign: both up (min) or both down (max), and $g_{xx}$ tells you which.

Step 6 in depth: Taylor Expansion

The 2nd-order Taylor expansion is the best quadratic "stand-in" for $g$ near a critical point — it tells you the local shape.

$$g(x,y)\approx g(x^*,y^*)+\tfrac12\big[g_{xx}(x-x^*)^2+2g_{xy}(x-x^*)(y-y^*)+g_{yy}(y-y^*)^2\big]$$
Why no linear terms?The first-order parts use $g_x$ and $g_y$ — but at a critical point those are both zero. So they vanish and the quadratic part leads.
Where the $\tfrac12$ comes fromTaylor's theorem divides the $n$-th term by $n!$. For the second-order term $n=2$, so $\tfrac{1}{2!}=\tfrac12$.
⚠️
Two easy marks people dropDon't forget the $\tfrac12$, and remember the cross-term coefficient is $2g_{xy}$ (not just $g_{xy}$).
Worked — Jan 2025 at the min $(1,1)$

$g(1,1)=1-3+1-3=-4$;   $g_{xx}=6,\ g_{yy}=6,\ g_{xy}=0$.

$$g\approx-4+\tfrac12\big[6(x-1)^2+6(y-1)^2\big]=-4+3(x-1)^2+3(y-1)^2$$

A bowl centred at $(1,1)$ sitting at height $-4$ — exactly what a local minimum should look like.

Domain: $x^2+y^2+1\geq1>0$ always → $D=\mathbb{R}^2$.
CPs: $g_x=\dfrac{2x}{(x^2+y^2+1)\ln10}=0\Rightarrow x=0$; likewise $y=0$. Only CP: $(0,0)$.
Classify: At $(0,0)$: $g_{xx}=\tfrac{2}{\ln10}>0,\ g_{xy}=0$, $\det H>0$ → GLOBAL MIN, $g(0,0)=0$.
Taylor: $g\approx \dfrac{x^2+y^2}{\ln10}$.
Contour map of $g=e^{x^2+y^2+1}$
CPs: $g_x=2x\,e^{x^2+y^2+1}=0\Rightarrow x=0$; $y=0$. Only CP: $(0,0)$.
Classify: At $(0,0)$: $g_{xx}=2e,\ g_{yy}=2e,\ g_{xy}=0$, $\det H=4e^2>0$ → GLOBAL MIN, $g(0,0)=e$.

Contour map of $g=\log(1-x^2-y^2)$
Domain: $1-x^2-y^2>0\Rightarrow x^2+y^2<1$ (open unit disk).
CPs: $g_x=\dfrac{-2x}{(1-x^2-y^2)\ln10}=0\Rightarrow x=0$; $y=0$. CP: $(0,0)$.
Classify: $g_{xx}=\tfrac{-2}{\ln10}<0$ at $(0,0)$, $\det H>0$ → GLOBAL MAX, $g(0,0)=0$.
Taylor: $g\approx -\dfrac{x^2+y^2}{\ln10}$.
Contour map of $g=x^2+y^2-2x-4y$
CPs: $g_x=2x-2=0\Rightarrow x=1$; $g_y=2y-4=0\Rightarrow y=2$. CP: $(1,2)$.
Classify: $H=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}$, $\det=4>0,\ g_{xx}>0$ → GLOBAL MIN, $g(1,2)=-5$.

⚡ Cheat Sheet

The morning of the exam, read only this. Twelve things to know cold, the traps that cost marks, and the time plan.

1
Matrix multiply: $(m\times n)(n\times p)=(m\times p)$. Entry $(i,j)$ = row $i$ DOT column $j$.
2
Log/Exp cancel: $e^{\ln x}=x$, $\ln(e^x)=x$, $\ln1=0$, $e^0=1$. Simplify these FIRST.
3
Domain rules: sqrt $\geq0$; log $>0$; fraction $\neq0$; $1/\ln$: inside $>0$ AND $\neq1$.
4
Quotient rule: $(u/v)'=(u'v-uv')/v^2$ — MINUS on top, square the bottom.
5
Product rule: $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$. Use it for $f''$ when $f'$ is a product.
6
Key derivatives: $[e^u]'=e^u u'$; $[\ln u]'=u'/u$; $[\log u]'=u'/(u\ln10)$.
7
Determinant shortcuts: Zero/identical row → $\det=0$. Triangular → product of diagonal.
8
1D min/max: $f'=0$ then $f''>0$ min, $f''<0$ max. Quadratic: $x^*=-b/2a$.
9
Exercise A order: Domain → Limits → $f'$ sign → $f''$ sign → Sketch.
10
Exercise B classify: $\det H>0,g_{xx}>0$ MIN; $\det H>0,g_{xx}<0$ MAX; $\det H<0$ SADDLE.
11
Taylor 2D: $g(x^*,y^*)+\tfrac12[g_{xx}\Delta x^2+2g_{xy}\Delta x\Delta y+g_{yy}\Delta y^2]$.
12
Separable: $g=f(x)+h(y)\Rightarrow g_{xy}=0$, diagonal $H$. 3×3 roots = 9 CPs.

The Traps That Cost Marks

❌
Not simplifying $\ln(e^x)=x$ before differentiatingFix: Simplify first — turns 2 minutes into 2 seconds.
❌
Plus instead of minus in the quotient ruleFix: It is $(u'v-uv')/v^2$. Minus on top.
❌
No product rule for $f''$Fix: $f'$ is almost always a product — both terms needed.
❌
Ignoring zero/identical rows in a determinantFix: They make $\det=0$ instantly — free marks.
❌
Missing critical points in Exercise BFix: 3 $x$-roots × 3 $y$-roots = 9 points. Find them all.
❌
Calling $\det>0$ a min without checking $g_{xx}$Fix: Need $\det>0$ AND $g_{xx}>0$ for a min.
❌
Dropping the $\tfrac12$ in TaylorFix: $g+\tfrac12[\ldots]$, and cross-term is $2g_{xy}$.
❌
Using $\geq$ for a log domainFix: Log needs strictly $>0$; sqrt allows $\geq0$.
